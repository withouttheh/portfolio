SpaceX: Falcon 9 Launch Analysis

The main goal of this project is to predict whether the Falcon 9 first stage will land successfully. SpaceX's reusability model allows them to advertise launches at $62M versus competitors' $165M+. Determining first-stage landing success allows us to estimate launch costs — useful for any company bidding against SpaceX.

The key question: For a given set of features about a Falcon 9 launch, will the first stage land successfully?

The methodology includes Data Collection, Data Wrangling, Exploratory Data Analysis, Data Visualization, and Machine Learning Prediction.

Can I predict whether the Falcon 9 first stage will land successfully?

Launch sites are strategically placed near the coast. Yellow markers show SpaceX launch site locations across the US.

Each site is close to a highway for logistics, near the coastline for safety testing, and maintains a safe distance from cities.

Success rate increases with the number of flights. Blue dots = successful launches, red = unsuccessful. A notable improvement is visible after the 40th launch at each site.

Payload mass (0–2500 kg vs 2500–5000 kg) shows minimal difference in success rate. The v1.1 booster shows the highest success rate across both ranges.

Orbits SSO, HEO, GEO, and ES-L1 achieve 100% success rates. SO orbit had 0% success.

Overall success improves year on year, with dips in 2018 and 2020.

Machine Learning

Four algorithms were evaluated to predict launch outcome. The Decision Tree classifier achieved the best accuracy at 94%.

Conclusion

There is a positive correlation between number of flights and success rate. Orbits SSO, HEO, GEO, and ES-L1 are most successful. Lighter payloads generally outperform heavier ones. Launch sites are strategically positioned for safety and logistics. The Decision Tree Classifier is the best predictive model for this dataset at 94% accuracy.
